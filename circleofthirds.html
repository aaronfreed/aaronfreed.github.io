Introduction

This is an analysis of the seven modes of the traditional major scale (whole step, whole step, half step, whole step, whole step, whole step, half step) and how they relate to each other. In brief, raising a specific pattern of notes in each scale results in cycling not merely through all twelve notes in the chromatic scale, but through all eighty-four possible sets of base notes and modes.

A brief (lol, lmao) explanation is perhaps in order: The seven modes I’m analyzing are arrangements of the notes of the major scale, traditionally numbered by which of the major scale’s notes they use as their root key, or start. Here are the C major scale’s modes. Lowercase, blue letters represent minor or diminished intervals that have been lowered a half-step from their root keys’ major scales; the bold, orange letter is an augmented interval that has been raised a half-step from its root key’s major scale. Note that an augmented fourth and a diminished fifth are both tritones; they just use different notations.

So why are these in a different order below? Because I’m analyzing how lowering a regular pattern of notes by a half-step each allows us to walk through every mode on every key. A few notes (pun intended):

  • A♯ and B♭ represent the same pitch. Likewise B and C♭, C♯ and D♭, and so on. However, in terms of music theory, A♯ and B♭ can be semantically quite different in some contexts – be careful.
  • Key signatures with seven sharps or seven flats exist, but I’ve omitted them. (There are even monstrosities with double-sharps [𝄪] and double-flats [𝄫] … and that’s all the attention I’ll be giving them.) I feel it was pretty extravagant just to include six-flat and six-sharp key signatures alongside each other.
  • If we start from Lydian mode, lowering pitches one at a time by a half-step each gives us:
    Modes Descending from Lydian
    #Mode Pitch Lowered
    1Ionian 4
    5Mixolydian7
    2Dorian 3

    • In other words, by lowering the B in F Lydian to B♭, we wind up with F Ionian (i.e., F major). Lowering the E in F major to E♭ gives us F Mixolydian. And so on.
    • Lowering the root from Locrian gives us Lydian mode for the key below it on the chromatic scale – i.e., lowering the B in B Locrian to B♭ gives us B♭ Lydian.
    • In the table above, the mode number and the pitch we lower to get it are exact mirrors of each other. This is not coincidental – it’s the direct result of a mathematical pattern.
    • The modes are also always either three steps on the scale below their predecessors (usually, but not always, a perfect fourth), or four steps above (usually, but not always, a perfect fifth). In this context, they’re equivalent, since the scale repeats every octave.
      • One pair of intervals always constitutes an exception to the “perfect fourths” and “perfect fifths” rules: one fourth is always augmented, and one fifth is always diminished. These are actually the same interval, a tritone. The following steps on each scale are tritones in either direction:

The Eighty-Four Modes of Major Scales

  • Modes of the C major scale are bolded and printed in blue to make them stand out. These are the only scales played using only the white keys of the piano (this is also why music notation can express these specific keys without the need for accidentals). The differences between their numbers are always multiples of twelve; this is also not a coincidence.
  • Halfway between C major’s modes, I always print a mode twice, in orange. These nodes could be notated either with six sharps in their key signatures or with six flats. I don’t really care for either option (E♯ is F♮! C♭ is B♮!), but they’re necessary to prevent the repetition of a letter in a scale, and thus a rash of accidentals in the notation for any piece that uses them. These options are also always separated by multiples of twelve, for the same reason: in this progression, modes of the same scale are always separated from each other by multiples of twelve.
  • I’ve also made a cheat sheet for what modes use what key signatures; see the bottom of this page for that.
  • I’ve listed these in descending order of pitch because that way, higher pitches are, well, higher on the list. However, since we read from top to bottom, this may be confusing. I may eventually write a JavaScript add-on to reverse the order, but I’d have to learn JavaScript first, so no promises.

4 - Lydian Maj Majmindim Majminmin

  • I’ve printed the root (I), dominant (V), and subdominant (IV) chords of each mode in bold – they are especially important to the mode’s tonality.
  • I’ve printed the seventh chord in lighter text because it’s diminished in the major scale.
  • Minor chords are blue; diminished chords are orange.
  • Printing the chords this way makes it clearer why Ionian became the standard major key and Aeolian became the natural minor key: the root, dominant, and subdominant are all major chords in Ionian mode and are are all minor chords in Aeolian mode. This makes them feel, respectively, especially major and especially minor.
  • Because their root chords are major, Lydian and Mixolydian still sound more “major” than the other modes (that aren’t Ionian), and Dorian and Phrygian still sound more “minor”. Locrian, as mentioned, is the oddball because it’s the only mode of the scale that uses a diminished chord as its root.

Yes, but, why?

I gazed into an eldritch dimension by writing a piece in Locrian mode. This was the result. I should’ve listened to Nietzsche’s warning about abysses.

(More seriously, my use of Locrian mode occasioned a discussion of what musical modes are, which in turn resulted in additional music theory discussion sent me down a rabbit hole of mathematical patterns. This is probably the HTML equivalent of the bulletin board board with string connecting pieces of a conspiracy, except the conspiracy is ultimately just that music and mathematics are low-key the same thing. If you don’t believe me, spend enough time studying calculus to understand Euler’s identity and you’ll see the music in math.)
