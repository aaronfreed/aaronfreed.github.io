Code Projects

I’ve made major contributions to the following code projects:

  • Scalatron ⟨github.com/aaronfreed/scalatron⟩
    • Rust library for analyzing musical scales
    • Input format: JSON table made up of scales, which must be named and must include either intervals, notes, or both
      • If interval count exceeds note count, fills in missing notes (rooted on C if none are specified)
      • If note count exceeds interval count, fills in missing intervals
    • Features extensive error checking and unit tests verifying that notes and intervals match
    • Currently outputs only HTML tables, although more output formats are planned
    • Additional planned features:
      • Transpose scale to specified key or to all twelve notes of chromatic scale
      • List all possible modes of scale, rooted on:
        • Root note of scale
        • Scale degree
        • All twelve notes, in “scale order”
        • All twelve notes, in “circle of fifths order” (my detailed, technical explanation ⟨aaronfreed.github.io/musicalmodes.html⟩)
      • Generate all possible modes of scale for all possible notes of chromatic scale. This requires filtering out duplicates for sixteen “modes of limited transposition”, as French composer Oliver Messiaen called them – see explanation here ⟨aaronfreed.github.io/musicalmodes.html#limitedtransposition⟩.
  • VAF ⟨github.com/aaronfreed/vaf⟩
    • A Lua texturing utility for Marathon Aleph One ⟨alephone.lhowon.org⟩, based on @Hopper262 ⟨github.com/Hopper262⟩ and Ares Ex Machina’s Va­sa­ra ⟨simplici7y.com/items/vasara⟩, which in turn is based on @jon­ir­ons ⟨github.com/jonirons⟩ and @tree­lla­ma’s ⟨github.com/treellama⟩ Visual Mode.lua ⟨github.com/treellama/visualmode⟩. (Visual Mode.lua is effectively the command line to Vasara’s GUI.)
    • When I began work on VAF, neither Vasara nor Visual Mode.lua had been substantially updated since 2016 (although both are now being updated again). As a result, I took it upon myself (with a bit of assistance from @Sol­ra­Biz­na ⟨github.com/SolraBizna⟩ and @mur­bruks­pro­dukt ⟨github.com/murbruksprodukt⟩) to fork Vasara to implement new Aleph One features, fix longstanding bugs, and add new features to make texturing easier.
    • Remains a work in progress, requires Aleph One 1.7 or later, and is best used with Weland ⟨github.com/treellama/weland/releases⟩. (See the readme for an abbreviated setup guide, or my beginners’ mapmaking guide ⟨aaronfreed.github.io/mapmaking101.html⟩ for a detailed one.)
    • Overall, VAF specifically caters to experienced mapmakers – Vasara’s UI is notably more minimalist (although this is partly because VAF is substantially more configurable and powerful). Beginning mapmakers may find it easier to start with Vasara and switch to VAF after getting a hang of the basics.
    • I haven’t updated this in a while; if my burnout over Marathon development ever recedes, I may do so again, but it’ll probably take several months at minimum.
  • Aleph Bet ⟨github.com/Aleph-Bet-Marathon/alephbet⟩
    • Aleph One fork with added features. Currently its in very early stages, but we have very ambitious, detailed future plans. Several others have done huge amounts of work on this, especially @Sol­ra­Biz­na ⟨github.com/SolraBizna⟩ and @Prism019 ⟨github.com/Prism019⟩.
    • Our initial 0.9 release is all but certain to include:
      • a unified build system ⟨mesonbuild.com⟩ across Windows, Linux, and MacOS (probably ≈95% implemented).
      • Solra and Nemo’s Second Music System (already coded, just not yet implemented).
    • Additional goals for 0.9, if we can complete them within a reasonable timeframe, are:
      • Yeet ffmpeg to an external binary, enabling hardware-accelerated rendering and more flexible encoding settings.
      • Add a way to edit controls in-game, without quitting to the menu.
    • The issues page ⟨github.com/Aleph-Bet-Marathon/alephbet/issues⟩ includes many, though by no means all, of our longer-term plans.
    • Currently on hold due to Solra’s health issues. Hopefully we’ll be able to resume this someday, but there’s a good chance that it’s dead.

Book-Length Works

  • On Moonlight: a film analysis, autobiography, & holistic societal critique ⟨aaronfreed.github.io/moonlight.html⟩ (≈82,000 words, 2017, 2025)

    Topics: film criticism, autobiography, neurodivergence, philosophy, ethics, political science, media analysis, historiography, psychology, mental illness, statistics

    • On 2017-03-11, I began writing commentary on the film Moonlight after one of my high school classmates won the Academy Award for Best Picture as its producer. I forgot to stop writing for the next six weeks, in the process delving into autobiography and holistic societal critique. I no longer agree with several things I wrote in it, and I consider it flawed in other ways as well; for instance, I spent too much time focusing on political differences without examining how those differences arose. It’s also quite dated; its political critique was vastly more relevant in 2017 than it is in 2025. Regardless, I think it can still do enough to help others that I’ve decided to exhibit it despite its flaws.
  • Aequilībrium harmoniae: a mathematical analysis of scales, modes, & the circle of fifths ⟨aaronfreed.github.io/musicalmodes.html⟩ (≈69,000 words, 2024-2025)

    Topics: music theory, music history, geometry, mathematical analysis, ancient Greece, Pythagoreans

    • Aequilībrium harmoniae means both The Harmony of Balance and The Balance of Harmony.
    • A lengthy, obsessively detailed, highly technical analysis (and history) of the diatonic major scale, its modes (Ionian, Dorian, Phrygian, Lydian, Mixolydian, Aeolian, and Locrian), their relationships to each other and the circle of fifths (a concept inextricably linked to chords, key signatures, and the diatonic major scale itself), its unique harmonic properties, and how those are inevitable mathematical results of its unusually even interval distribution. Contains dozens of tables illustrating the principle. Also examines pentatonic scales; variants of the diatonic scale; modes of limited transposition (rotational symmetry); reflective and translational symmetry; the Carnatic Numbered Mēḷakartā; the ancient Greek scales several of our modes are named after; why medieval Europeans accidentally reshuffled their names; the Fibonacci sequence’s surprising relationship to fundamental interval ratios; and the mathematical foundations of musical harmony that have remained effectively constant for nearly four millennia, including the diatonic major scale and the circle of fifths themselves, which are clearly described in surviving Mesopotamian tablets from ca. the 18th century BCE.
    • Was called Musical Modes and the Circle of Fifths until I decided that was too generic. Aequilībrium harmoniae sums up arguably its central theme: the diatonic major scale became standard because it’s the smallest possible scale to include three major triads (conveniently a perfect fifth apart, on IV, I, and V) and three minor triads (also conveniently a perfect fifth apart, on ii, vi, and iii); and it’s the most balanced and, therefore, harmonic seven-note scale possible in our tuning system. I briefly renamed it The Harmony of Balance until I decided the Latin genitive case, which conveys a reciprocal possessive relationship, fit its theme of balance better.
    • Appendix: All Scales in Twelve-Tone Equal Temperament ⟨aaronfreed.github.io/12tetscales.html⟩ (≈34,000 words)
      • All 2,048 modes of all 351 scales in 12-tone equal temperament, the main tuning system used in modern Western music. If taken as part of the book, this raises its overall word count to ≈102,000.

  • English Is a Germanic-Romance Language ⟨aaronfreed.github.io/germanic-romance.html⟩ (≈4,000 words)
    • My linguistic hot take: Old English is a completely different language from Modern English, while Middle English is simply an archaic dialect of Modern English. English changed vastly more between 1000 CE and 1400 CE than it did from 1400 CE to the present day. The Norman Conquest resulted in French influence that transformed English into a completely different language that we still speak today. Therefore, English should be classified as a Germanic-Romance language, not simply a Germanic one. I back this up with excerpts of Beowulf and The Canterbury Tales and exhaustive lists of English words that are derived from either Latin or Greek; I also show readers that they know more Greek than they probably think they do and contend that, for this reason, the idiom “it’s all Greek to me” should use a different language.
