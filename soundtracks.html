Remixes & Fan Soundtracks for Bungie’s Marathon (1994)

This is a list of (mostly) completed soundtracks for Bungie’s Marathon (1994) and its sequels, plus fan games for Aleph One, the open-source continuation of the Marathon engine. With the exception of Eternal X 1.3’s monster work-in-progress soundtrack, I’ve attempted to keep this predominantly to completed projects. (I’m still revising my 2022 remixes, but all sixteen tracks from the OST are already available; conversely, some twenty years later, I think it’s safe to say Tobacco or Cannibal Whore Feast are each highly unlikely to do additional remixes, and you do get all sixteen tracks of the OST between them.)

A quick note on file formats: FLAC is for listening outside the game, and MP3 or Ogg Vorbis for use within it (since Aleph One currently doesn’t play FLAC). ‘Pseudo-FLAC’ means I didn’t have a lossless source, so I made a facsimile. I explained my process in a document included with the Eternal 1.2 OST, which I’ve reproduced below with further elaboration on some specifics of the process.

I’ve endeavoured to list release dates as accurately as possible. All dates are ISO format (yyyy-mm-dd) where I possess information that specific; a few dates for which I have less specific information are listed as yyyy-mm or simply yyyy.

Marathon 1 Remixes

Craig Hardgrove’s site has a massive number of these. Except as noted, the following aren’t on his site, and unless otherwise noted, they all contain at least one remix of every track in Marathon 1’s OST:

  • QuickTime 2.0/2.5 stereo: YouTube, FLAC. QuickTime 2.0 in the left channel, QuickTime 2.5 in the right
  • My QuickTime 2.5 remixes (1997): YouTube, FLAC, Ogg Vorbis. Extremely faithful to the original sound, adding only stereo, reverb, and a few other flourishes
  • Tobacco (2001-07-24): pseudo-FLAC. Contains the following eight tracks:
    1. Chomber
    2. Fat Man
    3. Flowers in Heaven
    4. Landing
    5. Leela
    6. Rushing
    7. Splash (Marathon)
    8. Swirls
    Wonderful remakes that add some tasteful – and tasty – new instrumental parts.
  • Chibi-usa (2002-03-11): pseudo-FLAC. Tasteful reorchestrations with a higher-quality synthesizer.
  • Cannibal Whore Feast (2003-08-12): pseudo-FLAC. Contains the following eight tracks:
    1. Aliens Again
    2. Flippant
    3. Freedom
    4. Guardians
    5. New Pacific
    6. New Pacific (reprise)
    7. Rapture
    8. What About Bob?
    Like Chibi-usa’s, these are tasteful reorchestrations with a higher-quality synthesizer. And you can combine these with Tobacco’s for another complete set of remixes.
  • Will Christian (2016-10-17): SoundCloud, FLAC. Some interesting, inventive takes. I wouldn’t have thought to rearrange ‘Leela’ as a metal track, but… somehow, it works.
  • Talashar (2018-11-18): Bandcamp (streaming, FLAC, nearly any other format you want). Atmospheric reorchestrations that add new layers of harmony and melody, resulting in some fantastic twists on old classics.
  • Eternal X 1.2 OST (2018-12-21⁽¹⁾): pseudo-FLAC. Craig’s site has the Ogg Vorbis files (but if you have Eternal X 1.2 or 1.2.1, you already have most of these anyway) and a link to the YouTube video. These probably remain the most famous and acclaimed remakes of the OST.
  • CKT1138 (2021-03-12): YouTube, FLAC & Ogg Vorbis. Faithful reorchestrations using vintage ’80s and early ’90s synthesizers, with a sound worthy of Vangelis’ Blade Runner OST on several tracks.
  • Matrix_XV (2022-08-28): YouTube, pseudo-FLAC, Ogg Vorbis/MP3. Lush, generally faithful remakes with modern synthesizers, and the points of departure, like the Black Sabbath-inspired take on ‘Landing’, are inspired.
  • (WIP) My GarageBand remixes (ongoing since 2022-12-05): FLAC. ’70s/’80s-style remixes inspired by Marathon’s ‘used future’ aesthetic that take more creative liberties with the OST’s sound; still undergoing major revisions, but all sixteen tracks are already present. Roland drum machine and Mellotron samples are ubiquitous.
  • (WIP) Eternal X 1.3 OST (likely to be released sometime in 2023): FLAC/pseudo-FLAC (depending on track). More than five hours of music by eleven contributors, mostly drawing from the Marathon 1 soundtrack. There is some overlap with several of the above composers’ work, but since the tracks frequently segue seamlessly into one another, you probably won’t have heard most of these tracks in these exact configurations before (unless you’ve already played one of the recent Eternal 1.3 previews, of course).

Several others (i.e., wowbobwow, Storm, & dontask4470) have begun but not yet finished what I sincerely hope will be remixes of the entire OST. Search their names on the Discord and add ‘has: sound’ to bring them up.

Marathon 2 Special Edition OST

Pseudo-FLAC, MP3. If you follow the instructions below, the MP3 version will work with both the original Mac and the Windows 95 versions of the Marathon 2 map. (Aleph One does not play FLAC.)

Solo Lua scripts for use with the MP3 version only:

  • Unaltered M2SE tracklist
  • Revised tracklist: Reassigns the music on the following three levels, but is otherwise identical:
    • ‘God Will Sort the Dead’: the revised tracklist swaps out ‘Momar’, which the M2SE also uses on ‘Charon Doesn’t Make Change’, for ‘This Means War’, which the M2SE doesn’t use in any solo levels.
    • ‘The Big House’ and ‘Beware of Abandoned Rental Trucks’: the M2SE used the 90-second ‘Hopper’ on the former (which was greatly expanded and called ‘The Big House Special’ in the M2SE) and the 42-second ‘Dreamscape’ on the latter. The revised tracklist swaps these.

Select either of these and use them as a solo Lua script. Make sure you’ve got the MP3 files in a Tracks folder inside your Marathon 2 folder, that you don’t have any other solo Lua running, and that your music volume is something audible. So, if your Marathon 2 folder is:

F:\Games\Marathon 2

Then the path to ‘Alien Voyage.mp3’ should be:

F:\Games\Marathon 2\Tracks\Alien Voyage.mp3

Other paths will not work.

The Marathon 2 Special Edition itself is available here. See here for the Discord post where I originally posted these.

Remaster Info

Reproduced from Eternal X 1.2’s OST

My first step for around 75% of these was declipping. Many people seem to pay little attention to the levels when they mix and/or master songs, and as a result, the peaks and troughs of waveforms get clipped off (it’s essentially the result of a variable overflow), creating a form of distortion informally referred to as ‘black noise’. It results in a muffled sound and can cause listener fatigue. (If you want to find yourself annoyed at 90% of modern music, you can look up the loudness war.)

Thus, I employed iZotope’s declipper for any song that clipped. I must repeat that the declipper cannot perfectly reconstruct the data lost from clipping, but it usually comes up with reasonable approximations that will suffice for most people’s listening. The declipper scans the waveform and tells you where it clips. You can usually set the filter to start at 0.25 or 0.5 dB below that and get good results.

(I should note that in many cases, I recommend running this process an entire album at once so that you don’t wind up losing the relative levels of each song; there may be artistic reasons for the differences. However, in this case, I could reasonably infer that there wasn’t, so I did this process song-bysong. Additionally, note that on some albums, some songs may clip at different levels; for that matter, different segments of individual songs may also clip at different levels. Use your best judgement.)

Next, I ran most songs 90° through a phase rotation filter. This filter mostly affects subsonic (≤20 Hz) frequencies, but also usually results in higher dynamic range. I don’t have a hypothesis for why this reduces listener fatigue; it shouldn’t be perceptibly different at all. However, this is the only palatable way I’ve found to listen to various albums that are too distorted to salvage with declipping (i.e., Merzbow’s Venereology and Pulse Demon⁽⁴⁾; the Psychic Paramount’s Gamelan into the Mink Supernatural). Since it performed wonders for those recordings, it’s now a standard part of my remastering process.

Those two steps, along with normalising to -0.1 dB, should suffice for any recording for which you have a lossless source. Unfortunately, this was the case for none of the Eternal tracks; they were all MP3 files. Dr Hardgrove didn’t have higher-quality versions of his recordings; he said he might someday be able to make lossless versions, but it didn’t sound like it would be anytime soon. Thus, I approximated the missing upper frequencies as well. (I was unable to contact Nick⁽⁵⁾, Tommy, or Eike at all.)

In each case, I copied my results to a new file. Then I ran the “pitch & time” filter to raise the pitch by 12 half-steps, or an octave, using the highest possible quality algorithm (I think it’s called Radius). This is a time-consuming, processor-intensive task that may take several times a song or album’s running time on many machines. Feel free to step away to get a drink or something while it runs.

Once it finishes, roll off the lower frequencies of your pitch-raised copy with the corrective EQ filter. You’ll want to discard everything below about 14 kHz, depending upon the quality of MP3 compression. I used the analogue filter “low shelf” for most of the bands, but the exact slope and gain I used depended on the track. Lastly, mix paste the results with your original track, normalise to -0.1 dB, export as FLAC, and you’re done. [Note: In modern versions of iZotope, this filter is simply called ‘EQ’, and you can just use a high pass filter with it. I’ve also taken to adding a much more subtle low pass filter as well.]

This process may be able to salvage many modern recordings that you may otherwise find fatiguing. To be honest, I find myself putting about 90% of the music I download these days through at least some of these steps, but I admit to being obsessive about this sort of thing. Regardless, I hope some readers find it useful for some of the worst offenders.

Additional Notes

This pitch-shifting method works much better for some instruments than for others. Essentially, if an instrument is primarily based on even harmonics (meaning that if you a play a 440 Hz note, it will have resonant frequencies at 880 Hz, 1,760 Hz, 3,520 Hz, 7,040 Hz, 14,080 Hz, etc.), it will work almost flawlessly; if it is primarily based on odd harmonics, it won’t work nearly as well, though many listeners still probably won’t be able to notice since the above process results in most frequencies below 16 kHz being rolled off.

As it so happens, nearly every instrument prominent in rock music – drums, bass, guitar, organ, and piano – has fairly substantial even harmonics. This is less reliable for jazz and classical instruments – closed-end wind instruments tend to be based almost entirely on odd harmonics, so a clarinet will have almost no resonant frequencies an octave apart, while a trumpet will only have some. Synthesizers are a complete mixed bag; some synth voices have no even harmonics at all, while others have plenty. This Steve Hoffman Forums thread may be enlightening on this count.
